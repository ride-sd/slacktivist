# Information

Councilmember Campillo wants to ban children under 12 from riding Class 1 and Class 2 e-bikes — the pedal-assist kind that top out at 20 mph. The proposal goes before the Public Safety Committee, and the case for it doesn't hold up.

- **The evidence is a single TV news clip.** No SDPD collision data, no hospital breakdown, no analysis by e-bike class or rider age.
- **California already drew the line here.** State law restricts Class 3 e-bikes (28 mph) to riders 16+, but found no age minimum warranted for slower classes. San Diego would be going further without showing why.
- **The dangerous behaviors cited are already illegal.** Enforce existing law before adding new restrictions on kids riding responsibly.
- **Enforcement is unworkable.** Officers can't tell if a child is 11 or 12 — kids don't carry ID.
- **It conflicts with the City's Climate Action Plan.** Banning e-bikes for younger riders doesn't make those kids disappear — it makes them car passengers.

# Content

Dear Members of the Public Safety Committee,

I'm writing as a San Diego resident to ask you to oppose the proposed e-bike ordinance introduced by Councilmember Campillo. While I understand the stated goal is to improve safety, the ordinance — and the staff report behind it — don't demonstrate how its provisions would actually achieve that goal.

The evidence base is a single news article.

The staff report's entire factual foundation rests on one footnote: a link to a local TV news segment. There is no data from SDPD's Collision Investigation Bureau, no published hospital report, no breakdown by injury severity, and no analysis from any of the healthcare providers listed as stakeholders. For an ordinance that would restrict mobility for an entire age group, that is a remarkably thin evidentiary record.

The ordinance doesn't answer the questions that matter.

Even taking the injury trend at face value, the questions relevant to this specific ordinance are never asked — let alone answered. How many of those e-bike injuries involved Class 1 or Class 2 e-bikes, as opposed to Class 3 bikes, e-mopeds, or other motorized devices that often get lumped together in injury reports? Of the injuries that did involve Class 1 or Class 2 bikes, how many involved riders under 12 years old? Without answers to those questions, there is no logical path from "e-bike injuries are up" to "we should ban under-12s from Class 1 and Class 2 bikes."

This distinction matters because California law already draws a line based on speed and risk. Class 3 e-bikes, which reach 28 mph, already carry a minimum age of 16. Class 1 and Class 2 bikes top out at 20 mph — meaningfully slower, and comparable to a fast bicycle — and the Legislature did not impose an age minimum on them because it didn't find the evidence warranted one. If Councilmember Campillo believes San Diego data justifies going beyond what the state found necessary, that data needs to be presented, not assumed.

Existing laws already cover the dangerous behaviors described.

Running red lights, ignoring stop signs, riding without a helmet, riding dangerously on sidewalks — all of these are already illegal. If those behaviors are driving the injury numbers, the answer is enforcing the laws we have, not adding new ones that penalize kids who are riding responsibly.

The enforcement mechanism doesn't hold up to scrutiny.

Determining whether a rider is 11 or 12 years old is not straightforward — children in that age range don't carry ID. This provision would either go unenforced (in which case, why pass it?) or lead to subjective stops of kids who are doing nothing wrong. Neither outcome serves the stated safety goal, and both add burden to a police force that residents already say is stretched thin on traffic enforcement.

E-bikes provide real independence for kids — and San Diego's geography makes that especially true.

San Diego is not a flat city. For a child trying to get to school, visit a friend, or simply get around without relying on a parent, an e-bike may be the only realistic option when the alternative is a steep hill or a canyon crossing. Independent mobility at that age isn't a luxury — it helps address youth loneliness, reduces the burden on parents juggling multiple jobs and responsibilities, and builds the kind of self-sufficiency we should be encouraging in young people. Restricting that independence for an age group that has not been shown to be the source of the safety problem does real harm to real kids, and the ordinance doesn't reckon with that tradeoff at all.

The ordinance works against goals the City has already committed to.

The staff report itself acknowledges that e-bikes support the City's Climate Action Plan by reducing greenhouse gas emissions and vehicle miles traveled. Restricting e-bike access for younger riders doesn't make those kids disappear — it makes them car passengers instead. That is in direct tension with the City's own sustainability commitments, and the staff report offers no explanation for how those goals are being balanced.

What did the community actually say?

The staff report lists "parents and community members" among the stakeholders consulted, but provides no specifics and no summary of what was heard. Which parent groups were contacted? Which community organizations? Were any cycling advocates, e-bike retailers, or active transportation groups consulted? And critically — what did those stakeholders actually say? Did they support this ordinance, or did they raise concerns? A staff report that gestures at community outreach without disclosing who was consulted or what they said gives the Committee no way to assess whether this proposal reflects genuine community support. I'd ask that a full accounting of that feedback — including any opposition — be provided before this moves any further.

I'd ask you to table this ordinance until the District can present actual injury data broken down by e-bike class and rider age, and can explain specifically how an under-12 ban on Class 1 and Class 2 bikes reduces those injuries. As written, this is a solution in search of a problem.

Thank you for your time and consideration.

# Information

The Public Safety Committee passed Councilmember Campillo's e-bike ordinance unanimously (4–0). It now goes to the **full City Council on Tuesday, June 23 at 10:00 a.m. as Item S501** (Ordinance O-2026-142, adding Municipal Code §§ 84.19–84.21). It sits on the **consent agenda for introduction** — meaning it can be adopted without any discussion unless a councilmember pulls it. This is the moment to weigh in.

**What the ordinance actually does:** It bans children under 12 from riding Class 1 and Class 2 e-bikes — the slowest category, capped at 20 mph — with a $25 ticket that can be waived by taking a safety course. It also limits passengers to bikes with a dedicated seat.

**Why it aims at the wrong target:**

- **It does nothing about the bikes people are actually worried about.** The alarming headlines are about e-motos (Sur-Rons, Talarias) and Class 3 e-bikes hitting 28 mph — ridden fast, often against traffic. This ordinance touches none of that. E-motos aren't even e-bikes under California law; they're unregistered motor vehicles already illegal for unlicensed kids to ride. Class 3 bikes already carry a statewide minimum age of 16. The ordinance adds nothing to either.
- **It regulates the opposite end of the spectrum.** Class 1 and Class 2 bikes top out at 20 mph — the slowest there is — and the ban falls on the youngest riders on them.
- **The evidence is thin.** The staff report's factual basis is a local TV news segment — no SDPD collision data, no hospital breakdown, and no analysis by e-bike class or rider age.
- **It's unenforceable.** An officer can't tell whether a child is 11 or 12, and kids that age don't carry ID.
- **It works against the City's own goals.** San Diego is hilly; for many kids an e-bike is the realistic way to get to school without a parent driving them. Banning the slowest bikes for the youngest riders turns those trips into car trips — in tension with the City's Climate Action Plan.

# Content

Dear Councilmembers,

I'm a San Diego resident writing to ask you to strike the under-12 provision from the e-bike ordinance before you on June 23 (Item S501). Stripped of its safety framing, this provision does one concrete thing: it takes a reasonable, independent way of getting around away from young children. Councilmember Campillo has built his public case on the frightening image of fast, reckless e-bike riding — but he has been notably cagey about the fact that what his ordinance actually restricts is the slowest bikes ridden by the youngest kids, while leaving the genuinely dangerous machines untouched.

This ordinance does nothing about the vehicles people are actually worried about.

When residents hear "e-bike crackdown," they picture the machines behind the alarming headlines: e-motos like the Sur-Ron and Talaria, and Class 3 e-bikes that hit 28 mph — ridden fast, often by teenagers, frequently against traffic. This ordinance touches none of that. E-motos aren't even e-bikes under California law; they're unregistered motor vehicles that already require a license and are already illegal for kids to ride on public streets. Class 3 e-bikes already carry a statewide minimum age of 16. The ordinance adds nothing to either. What it actually regulates is the opposite end of the spectrum — Class 1 and Class 2 bikes, capped at 20 mph, the slowest category there is — and the youngest riders on them. It lets the public believe the City is going after the 35-mph dirt bike while it is really ticketing an 11-year-old on a 20-mph pedal bike.

The evidence doesn't support an under-12 ban specifically.

The staff report's factual basis is a local TV news segment — no SDPD collision data, no hospital breakdown, and critically, no analysis by e-bike class or rider age. Before restricting an entire age group, the City should be able to answer two questions: how many of these injuries involved Class 1 or Class 2 bikes, rather than the Class 3 bikes and e-motos that routinely get lumped together in injury counts? And of those, how many involved riders under 12? Those questions are never asked, let alone answered. There is no path from "e-bike injuries are up" to "ban under-12s from the slowest bikes."

The ban is unenforceable, and it falls on the wrong kids.

An officer can't tell whether a child is 11 or 12, and kids that age don't carry ID. This provision will either go unenforced — in which case, why pass it — or it will invite subjective stops of children doing nothing wrong. Meanwhile, the dangerous behaviors everyone actually objects to — running red lights, riding against traffic, riding without a helmet — are already illegal regardless of the rider's age or the class of bike.

What this ordinance actually takes away.

For a child in a hilly city like San Diego, a Class 1 or Class 2 e-bike is often the only realistic way to get to school, to a friend's house, or to practice without an adult driving them there and back. That independence is not a luxury — it is how kids learn to navigate their own city, and it lifts a real burden off working parents. Councilmember Campillo's ordinance removes that for every child under 12, and the way he has sold it — heavy on the danger of e-bikes in the abstract, quiet on the slow bikes and young riders it actually reaches — keeps the public from seeing that tradeoff clearly. That gap is not incidental. The danger people picture comes from e-motos and Class 3 bikes this ordinance does nothing about; the riders it does reach are 11-year-olds on 20-mph bikes. The Council should not pass a ban whose real effect is so different from the way it is being presented.

It also works against the City's own commitments. Banning the slowest e-bikes for the youngest riders doesn't make their trips disappear; it turns them into car trips — in direct tension with the Climate Action Plan, which the staff report itself credits e-bikes for advancing.

If the Council wants to make e-bike riding safer, the answer is to focus enforcement on the high-speed vehicles that are already illegal for kids to ride — e-motos and Class 3 bikes — not to ticket the youngest riders on the slowest bikes. I urge you to strike the under-12 provision. At a minimum, please pull Item S501 from the consent agenda so it receives a real public hearing rather than passing without discussion.

Thank you for your time and consideration.

San Diego resident
